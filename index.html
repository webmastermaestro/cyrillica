What is the Cyrillic script?

The Cyrillic script is an alphabet used for writing several languages across Eastern Europe and Northern and Central Asia. Named after the Byzantine monk Saint Cyril, who, along with his brother Methodius, created it in the 9th century, the script originally aimed to translate religious texts into the Slavic languages. Over time, it spread and adapted to suit various languages, including Russian, Ukrainian, Bulgarian, Serbian, and many others. Its distinctive characters often feature angular shapes and curves, with some letters unique to specific languages. Today, Cyrillic remains a vital element of cultural identity for millions of people and serves as a key tool for communication, education, and literature in its diverse range of linguistic contexts.
